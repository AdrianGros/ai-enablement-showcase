# AI Enablement Showcase Deck Brief

## Working Title

Kuenstliche Intelligenz im Arbeitsalltag: Moeglichkeiten, Grenzen und verantwortliche Nutzung

## Presentation Goal

Build a public-facing showcase deck that demonstrates:

- AI enablement competence
- clear explanation of complex topics for non-specialists
- practical workplace guidance
- responsible framing of risk and governance

## Final Audience

Primary audience:

- non-specialists in company environments
- technically interested employees without AI deep expertise
- team members exploring practical AI usage at work

Secondary audience:

- project managers
- team leads
- business-side multipliers and change agents

## Final Learning Goals

By the end of the talk, the audience should be able to:

1. explain in simple terms how modern generative AI works at a functional level
2. name realistic strengths and limitations of current AI systems
3. identify useful workplace use cases for AI in knowledge work
4. apply a simple method for getting better results from AI systems
5. recognize why guardrails, review, and human accountability matter

## Recommended Slide Count

12 slides total.

This is enough to create a clear narrative without turning the deck into a general AI lecture.

## Slide Architecture

### 1. Title

Main message:

AI is already part of work. The useful question is how to use it well and responsibly.

Recommended slide type:

- title slide with subtitle

Content blocks:

- main title
- subtitle
- optional short presenter line

### 2. Why This Matters Now

Main message:

Generative AI is increasingly used for drafting, summarizing, and information support, so practical usage and review habits matter.

Recommended slide type:

- relevance slide with 3-part tension structure

Content blocks:

- why people use AI now
- what goes wrong without method
- why this session matters

### 3. What AI Is Good At

Main message:

Current generative AI systems are useful for drafting, summarizing, transforming, and brainstorming text-heavy work.

Recommended slide type:

- capability overview

Content blocks:

- summarize
- rewrite
- structure
- compare
- brainstorm

### 4. What AI Is Not Good At

Main message:

Generative AI can produce fluent but inaccurate output, so users should not assume correctness without verification.

Recommended slide type:

- contrast slide

Content blocks:

- no real understanding
- can invent details
- weak on unstated context
- weak if unchecked

### 5. How It Works at a Useful Level

Main message:

Large language models generate text by predicting likely token sequences from patterns learned during training data.

Recommended slide type:

- simple process diagram

Content blocks:

- trained on large amounts of examples
- predicts likely next tokens
- output quality depends heavily on prompt and context

### 6. Where It Helps in Work

Main message:

A practical starting point is support work around drafting, summarization, preparation, and restructuring in knowledge work.

Recommended slide type:

- applied examples slide

Content blocks:

- drafting and rewriting
- research preparation
- meeting and note synthesis
- planning and structuring
- communication support

### 7. Good Usage Is a Method

Main message:

Using explicit context, role, goal, examples, and review tends to improve output quality.

Recommended slide type:

- 5-step framework slide

Content blocks:

1. context
2. role
3. goal
4. examples
5. check

This is one of the core showcase slides and should be visually prominent.

### 8. What Goes Wrong in Practice

Main message:

Weak instructions and lack of review increase the chance of generic, irrelevant, or incorrect output.

Recommended slide type:

- failure-pattern slide

Content blocks:

- vague request
- missing business context
- no examples
- no review
- copy-paste without ownership

### 9. Why Agents Are a Different Risk Class

Main message:

AI systems that can trigger tools or external actions require stronger safeguards, oversight, and limits than chat-only use.

Recommended slide type:

- escalation or risk ladder

Content blocks:

- chatbot answers
- agent can act
- actions amplify mistakes
- more autonomy requires more control

The ClawBot idea should be generalized here as an example of action-capable AI, not as a private project deep dive.

### 10. Guardrails for Responsible Use

Main message:

Responsible use benefits from defined boundaries, human review, and clear rules for higher-impact tasks.

Recommended slide type:

- guardrail framework

Content blocks:

- no sensitive data by default
- verify important outputs
- define allowed and disallowed use
- escalate when impact rises

### 11. Three Rules for Everyday Use

Main message:

Useful everyday AI use depends on context, verification, and human accountability.

Recommended slide type:

- rule-of-three closing slide

Content blocks:

- AI is a tool, not truth
- quality needs context
- responsibility stays with people

### 12. Conclusion and Q&A

Main message:

AI is most useful when applied with clear prompts, review, and appropriate guardrails.

Recommended slide type:

- recap and invitation to discussion

Content blocks:

- one-line summary
- optional prompt for questions

## Optional Compression Path

If the deck must shrink to 10 slides:

- merge slides 3 and 4 into one strengths-versus-limits slide
- merge slides 10 and 11 into one guardrails-and-rules slide

## Design Rules

- use 16:9 widescreen
- use a simple sans serif font family
- keep body text at 18 pt or larger
- one main idea per slide
- keep most slides to 3 to 5 short bullets or one simple visual structure
- prefer process diagrams, comparisons, or grouped examples over dense text blocks
- maintain strong contrast between text and background
- use built-in layouts and consistent title placement
- give every slide a unique title
- avoid decorative tables unless truly necessary
- do not rely on color alone to convey meaning
- if icons or illustrations are used, keep them functional and consistent

## What To Avoid

- long definitions at the start
- technical deep dives into model architecture
- vendor marketing language
- fear-driven risk framing
- crowded slides
- copied paragraphs as slide text
- private company examples or sensitive scenarios

## Priority Slides

Highest-value slides for the later build:

1. slide 2: relevance and framing
2. slide 7: 5-step method
3. slide 9: agents as a different risk class
4. slide 11: three rules for everyday use

## Narrative Logic

The deck should follow this path:

1. relevance before definition
2. capability before mechanism
3. mechanism before method
4. method before risk
5. risk before guardrails
6. guardrails before memorable close

That sequence keeps the presentation practical, non-technical, and credible for AI enablement.
